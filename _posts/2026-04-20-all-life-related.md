title: How do we know that all life on earth is related?
---

A very reasonable question. Evolutionary biologists like to drop this fact as if it's obvious, and in a sense it is once you know what you're looking at. But it can be tricky to explain from first principles. Let's use my favourite analogy.

Imagine we take a trip to a museum (in my imagination this is always the National Museum of Scotland in Edinburgh).  In the museum there are lots of different rooms with different artefacts. And let's imagine that we walk into a room that's full of all different kinds of boats. 

For the purposes of this story it doesn't matter whether they're original boats or reproductions but they're all ancient, very old boats from thousands of years ago. So we have to imagine we're walking through this room and we see a dugout canoe from the Netherlands and a reed boat from South America and an animal skin covered canoe from somewhere in North America and a Polynesian outrigger and all sorts of other ancient types of boat.

 We wander through the room looking at the different kinds of boats and then notice a plaque on the wall - you know, the usual explanatory sign that you get in any room in a museum. This plaque says that these are all examples of boats from ancient cultures. And it goes on to note that all of these boats are so old that they were all developed before any of these cultures had any contact with each other. In other words, all of the boats were built by people who had never seen any of the other types of boat.

Now, imagine we stop and look back at the boats and wonder if this is really true, because all of the boats look kind of similar. They're all long and narrow in one dimension (except the coracle) and they're all hollow. They've all got at least one pointy end (except the coracle). Some have two pointy ends. They've all got a fairly smooth surface. 

And if we look at the other bits, they all seem very similar too. For the boats that have oars, the oars are long and have thin handles and broad-bladed paddles. For the ones that have sails, the sails are made out of something lightweight and they have a big surface area, and they're generally put up on some sort of pole. 

So we may be forgiven for doubting whether the sign is true - were all of these boats really built completely independent of each other? Because if none of the builders of these boats had ever seen any of the other boats, how come they all ended up with the same design?

The straightforward answer is that the boats look the same because of physics; because there's one natural design that works especially well for things that need to move through water. Things that move through the water have to be smooth, so they don't create a lot of drag, and narrow in one direction for the same reason. If those things are going to stay on the surface of the water, then they need to be hollow or light. If they're going to carry useful things, like people or cargo, then it's better for them to be hollow inside, so that you can fit cargo in. 

The same argument applies for things like the oars. Oars work best if they're long (so they can act as a lever to amplify human muscle power) and if they have broad blades at the end (so that they can move a lot of water). Sails work best if they're large (to catch a lot of air) but not too heavy, and if they are raised up a bit (to do a better job of catching the wind). So there are good objective reasons for all of these things to end up looking the same.

When we look at the animal world, we see similar patterns. If we look at fish and dolphins and squid and seals, they all have kind of similar shapes. They've all got smooth bodies and at least one pointy end to move through the water efficiently. And if they've got fins or flippers, they tend to be shaped a little bit like oars - they are broad and flat to be able to move a lot of water around.

For this reason, we can't look at similarities between, for example, sharks and dolphins, and conclude that they must be closely related because they look similar. They look similar not because they're closely related but because they look the way that their bodies have to look to move efficiently through the water. Like the boats, they have the same shape because of physics. Famously, this is called convergent evolution by people in the business.

Incidentally, we can tell that sharks and dolphins are not closely related as soon as you look at the tail fins because on sharks they go up and down, and on dolphins they go left to right. And that's even before you think about the fact that dolphins breathe air.

Now let's imagine ourselves back in that room in the museum. Same boats, same sign on the wall that says all of these boats were developed completely independently. But this time after reading the sign we go back for a closer look at the boats and we notice that they all have names, painted or carved into the side or the back. Now that's not unusual in itself - we could easily imagine that many cultures have independently come up with the idea of naming their boats. But imagine we look closer and we see that all the names are written in the same language - they use the same alphabet. And to take it a step further, let's imagine that we can read the script and that all of the boats have exactly the same name.

In your imagination, you can imagine whatever name you like for the boat, it doesn't make any difference to the analogy. But immediately we can see that boats having the same name written in the same script is something fundamentally different to having similar shapes. We're not surprised when boats have similar shapes independently, because the shapes are the best for moving through water. But there's no sense in which one name for a boat is better than another. So if we actually see this feature on all of the boats, then we would have to conclude that the sign is incorrect - that the boats were not built completely independently, that all of these names came from a common source. It's just far too unlikely that a bunch of boat builders all independently came up with the same alphabet to represent the sounds of their language, and then all came up independently with the exact same name. So we would have to conclude that these boats had either been copied from one another or copied from some other, even older boat design.

It turns out that when we look at all the different forms of life that we have so far discovered, we see something identical to the situation with the names of the boats. All living things share certain similarities that are way too unlikely to have occurred by chance, and that can't be explained by simply being the "best" way of doing things, as our marine animals have the "best" body shape for moving through water. 

There are a few different specific examples, but the most compelling one is the genetic code - the set of rules that turn genetic information stored in DNA into the chemicals that make up cells and make them work. This is astonishingly consistent across plants, animals, fungi and bacteria, with only tiny, rare differences. But it's also kind of arbitrary - there's no sense in which the code that living things actually use is the "best" one, many other variations would work just as well. So it's like the names of the boats; the only reasonable explanation is that the code is the same in all living things because it was inherited from the same ancestor. 

Other universal similarities exist like the use of DNA for storing genetic information, ribosomes for making proteins, ATP for storing energy, left- and right-handed versions of amino acids and sugars. But this post is already long enough....
